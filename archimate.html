General Definitions

ArchiMate

ArchiMate Enterprise Architecture modeling language, a visual language with a set of default iconography for describing, analyzing, and communicating many concerns of Enterprise Architectures as they change over time. The standard provides a set of entities and relationships with their corresponding iconography for the representation of Architecture Descriptions.

Enterprise Architecture

The practice of analyzing, designing, planning, and implementing enterprise analysis to successfully execute on business strategies.

Visual Language

A type of language that uses visual elements to convey meaning.

ArchiMate Definitions

ArchiMate Core Framework

A reference structure used to classify elements of the ArchiMate core language. It consists of three layers and three aspects.

ArchiMate Core Language

The central part of the ArchiMate language that defines the concepts to model Enterprise Architectures. It includes concepts from three layers: Business, Application, and Technology (including Physical).

Architecture View

A representation of a system from the perspective of a related set of concerns.

Architecture Viewpoint

A specification of the conventions for a particular kind of architecture view.

Aspect

Classification of elements based on layer-independent characteristics related to the concerns of different stakeholders. Used for positioning elements in the ArchiMate metamodel.

Attribute

A property associated with an ArchiMate language element or relationship.

Model

A model contains elements and relationships within an architecture.

Concept

A conceptual entity within the ArchiMate modeling language.

Element

A fundamental building block within an architecture model.

CompositeElement

An element that does not necessarily fit in a single aspect of the framework but may combine two or more aspects.

Active Structure

A category of elements that perform behavior and collaborate to provide services.

External Active Structure

A category of external active structure elements (also called interfaces) that perform behavior and collaborate to provide services.

Internal Active Structure

A category of internal elements that perform behavior and collaborate to provide services.

Behavior

Represents elements that define the behavior performed by active structure elements.

External Behavior

Represents external elements (also called services) that define the behavior performed by active structure elements.

Internal Behavior

Represents internal elements that define the behavior performed by active structure elements.

Event

Represents elements that define the behavior performed by active structure elements.

Passive Structure

Represents structural elements that are used or produced by behavior elements.

Layer

A major division in the architecture that categorizes elements according to their scope and purpose.

Business Layer

Represents elements and relationships that describe the organization's business functions and processes.

Application Layer

Represents elements and relationships that describe the organization's business functions and processes.

Relationship

A relationship between two elements within an architecture model.

Relationship Connector

A connector element that connects relationships between elements.

Structural Relationship

A structural relationship defines the static construction or composition of concepts.

Dependency Relationship

A dependency relationship models how elements depend on each other.

Dynamic Relationship

A dynamic relationship models behavioral dependencies between elements.

Other Relationship

Other types of relationships not covered by structural, dependency, or dynamic relationships.

Elements

Business Actor

An organizational entity that is capable of performing behavior.

Business Role

The responsibility for performing specific behavior, to which an actor can be assigned.

Business Collaboration

An aggregate of two or more business roles that work together to perform collective behavior.

Business Interface

A point of access where a business service is made available to the environment.

Business Process

A sequence of business behaviors that achieve a specific outcome.

Business Function

A collection of business behavior based on a chosen set of criteria (typically required skills, resources, etc.) and closely aligned to an organization.

Business Service

An explicitly defined exposed business behavior.

Business Interaction

A unit of collective business behavior performed by two or more business actors, business roles, or business collaborations.

Business Event

Represents a business-related state change.

Business Object

Represents a concept used within a particular business domain.

Business Constract

Represents a formal or informal specification of an agreement between a provider and a consumer that specifies the rights and obligations associated with a product and establishes functional and non-functional parameters for interaction.

Representation

A perceptible form of the information carried by a business object.

Product

Represents a coherent collection of services and/or passive structure elements, accompanied by a contract, which is offered as a whole to (internal or external) customers.

Application Component

An encapsulation of application functionality aligned to implementation structure, which is modular and replaceable.

Application Collaboration

Represents an aggregate of two or more application internal active structure elements that work together to perform collective application behavior.

Application Interface

Represents a point of access where application services are made available to a user, another application component, or a node.

Application Function

Represents an automated behavior that can be performed by an application component.

Application Interaction

an encapsulation of application functionality aligned to implementation structure, which is modular and replaceable.

Application Process

Addresses a set of related concerns and is tailored for specific stakeholders. A View is specified by means of a Viewpoint, which prescribes the concepts, models, analysis techniques, and visualizations that are provided by the View. Application Service

Data Object

Represents data structured for automated processing.

Node

A computational or physical resource that hosts, manipulates, or interacts with other computational or physical resources.

Device

A physical IT resource upon which system software and artifacts may be stored or deployed for execution.

System Software

Software that provides or contributes to an environment for storing, executing, and using software or data deployed within it.

Technology Collaboration

An aggregate of two or more technology internal active structure elements that work together to perform collective technology behavior.

Technology Interface

A technology collaboration represents an aggregate of two or more technology internal active structure elements that work together to perform collective technology behavior.

Path

A link between two or more technology internal active structure elements, through which these elements can exchange data, energy, or material.

Communication Network

A set of structures that connects devices or system software for transmission, routing, and reception of data.

Technology Function

A collection of technology behavior that can be performed by a technology internal active structure element.

Technology Process

A sequence of technology behaviors that achieves a specific result.

Technology Interaction

A unit of collective technology behavior performed by (a collaboration of) two or more technology internal active structure elements.

Technology Event

A technology event represents a technology state change.

Technology Service

An explicitly defined exposed technology behavior.

Artifact

A piece of data that is used or produced in a software development process, or by deployment and operation of an IT system.

Relationships

Assignment

Links active structure elements with units of behavior that are performed by them, or roles that are fulfilled by them.

Aggregation

Models a whole-part relationship between a composite and its parts.

Composition

Indicates that an element consists of one or more other elements.

Realization

Indicates that an entity (e.g., a business process or function) realizes another entity (e.g., a business service).

Serving

Models that an element provides its functionality to another element.

Access

Models the access of a behavior element to a passive structure element.

Read

Indicates that a behavior element reads or retrieves information from a passive structure element.

Write

Indicates that a behavior element writes or modifies information in a passive structure element.

Read-Write

Indicates that a behavior element both reads from and writes to a passive structure element.

Influence

Models how an element influences another element.

Sign

Indicates the sign of the influence (e.g., positive or negative).

Strength

Indicates the strength of the influence (e.g., weak, moderate, strong).

Association

Models a relationship between objects that is not covered by the other relationship types.

Triggering

Models the temporal or causal relationship between two behavior elements, where one triggers the other.

Flow

The flow relationship represents transfer from one element to another.

Specialization

Indicates that an element is a more specific form of another element of the same type.

Key Principles Applied

1. OWL-Only Semantic Definitions (ontology/ directory)

What goes here: Universal truths, inference rules, and semantic relationships that apply globally across all ArchiMate models.

Key characteristics:

  • owl:TransitiveProperty for specialization and composition
  • owl:FunctionalProperty for unique identifiers
  • owl:propertyChainAxiom for derived relationships
  • Domain and range restrictions that represent universal truths
  • No context-specific business rules

Example from current file:

# Universal truth: Specialization is transitive
	<#specialization> rdf:type owl:ObjectProperty ;
	    rdf:type owl:TransitiveProperty ;
	    rdf:type owl:AsymmetricProperty .
	
	# Universal truth: Assignment chains with serving create indirect serving
	<#indirectServing> owl:propertyChainAxiom ( <#assignment> <#serving> ) .

2. SHACL-Only Validation Rules (validation/ directory)

What goes here: Context-specific constraints, business rules, and data quality requirements that vary by use case.

Core Validation (validation/core/)

  • Universal structural constraints (no self-relationships, unique IDs)
  • Basic ArchiMate metamodel compliance
  • Relationship domain/range validation

Context-Specific Validation (validation/context/)

  • Business Analyst: Business layer completeness, stakeholder linkage, AI extraction confidence
  • Technical Architect: Deployment constraints, performance requirements, security validation
  • Data Architect: Data lineage, storage requirements, integration patterns
  • Enterprise: Cross-layer relationships, governance requirements

Continuum Validation (validation/maturity/)

  • For a folder that holds time and space descriptions. Describes the Enterprise "In-motion"
  • In classic physics term, describes how two objects cannot occupy the same space at the same time
  • Optional human-readable label: “spatial-temporal exclusion” or “mutual exclusivity.”

Quality Assurance (validation/quality/)

  • Naming conventions by role and context
  • Documentation completeness requirements
  • AI-generated content validation thresholds

Migration Strategy from Your Current File

Step 1: Extract OWL Semantics

Move these sections to archimate-core-ontology.ttl:

  • All owl:Class definitions (keep semantic meaning only)
  • owl:ObjectProperty definitions with OWL characteristics
  • Property chain axioms and inference rules
  • Universal domain/range restrictions

Step 2: Extract SHACL Validation

Move these sections to appropriate validation files:

  • All sh:NodeShape definitions
  • SPARQL-based validation queries
  • Access type, influence sign validations
  • Relationship uniqueness constraints

Step 3: Separate by Context

Distribute SHACL rules based on:

  • User role (BA, TA, DA)
  • Maturity level (basic, temporal, operational, geospatial, enterprise)
  • Quality concern (naming, completeness, AI confidence)

Step 4: Add RDF-Star Relationship Metadata

Create separate files for:

  • Relationship attribute definitions (accessType, influenceSign, etc.)
  • RDF-Star specific validation rules
  • Metadata quality constraints

Benefits of This Structure

1. Modularity

  • Each file has a single, clear purpose
  • Easy to maintain and update independently
  • Supports your layered user strategy

2. Reusability

  • Core ontology can be shared across different validation contexts
  • Validation rules can be mixed and matched for different scenarios
  • Supports your federated architecture approach

3. Maintainability

  • Changes to business rules don't affect semantic definitions
  • Context-specific validation can evolve independently
  • Clear separation supports your prototype development

Required Attributes for All Realized Elements and Relationships

Following your specification, every realized element and relationship must have these four attributes:

Core Ontology Definition (OWL)

# Universal semantic constraint - all concepts need specialization
	<#specialization> rdf:type owl:ObjectProperty ;
	    rdfs:label "specialization" ;
	    rdfs:comment "Links realized instances to their abstract ArchiMate types" ;
	    rdfs:domain <#Concept> ;
	    rdfs:range <#Concept> ;
	    rdf:type owl:FunctionalProperty .

Validation Rules (SHACL)

# All realized elements must have: identifier, name, documentation, specialization
	ex:RealizedElementShape rdf:type sh:NodeShape ;
	    sh:targetClass archimate:Element ;
	    sh:property [
	        sh:path archimate:identifier ;
	        sh:minCount 1 ; sh:maxCount 1 ;
	        sh:datatype xsd:string ;
	        sh:pattern "^[a-zA-Z_][a-zA-Z0-9_-]*$"
	    ] ;
	    sh:property [
	        sh:path archimate:name ;
	        sh:minCount 1 ; sh:maxCount 1 ;
	        sh:datatype xsd:string ; sh:minLength 1
	    ] ;
	    sh:property [
	        sh:path archimate:documentation ;
	        sh:minCount 1 ;
	        sh:datatype xsd:string ; sh:minLength 1
	    ] ;
	    sh:property [
	        sh:path archimate:specialization ;
	        sh:minCount 1 ;
	        sh:class archimate:Element
	    ] .

Example Usage

# Abstract ArchiMate type (defined in ontology)
	archimate:BusinessActor rdf:type owl:Class ;
	    rdfs:label "Business Actor" ;
	    rdfs:subClassOf archimate:ActiveStructure, archimate:BusinessLayer ;
	    skos:exactMatch archimate-skos:BusinessActor .
	
	# Realized instance (in actual models)
	ex:SalesManager rdf:type archimate:Element ;
	    archimate:identifier "sales-mgr-001" ;
	    archimate:name "Sales Manager" ;
	    archimate:documentation "Responsible for regional sales operations and team management" ;
	    archimate:specialization archimate:BusinessActor .
	
	# Realized relationship instance
	ex:SalesManagerToSalesProcess rdf:type archimate:Relationship ;
	    archimate:identifier "rel-001" ;
	    archimate:name "Sales Manager performs Sales Process" ;
	    archimate:documentation "Sales Manager is assigned to perform the Sales Process" ;
	    archimate:specialization archimate:assignment ;
	    # RDF-Star metadata for the relationship
	    rdf:subject ex:SalesManager ;
	    rdf:predicate archimate:assignment ;
	    rdf:object ex:SalesProcess .
